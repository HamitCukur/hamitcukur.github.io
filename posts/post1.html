why engineers should read marcus aurelius

01-01-2024

in an age where instant gratification reigns supreme and social media dictates our worth, the ancient wisdom of marcus aurelius hits different. his words, written nearly two millennia ago, speak directly to the chaos of our modern lives. üòè

here are three timeless principles from the philosopher king that we desperately need today:

1. focus on what you can control
"you have power over your mind - not outside events. realize this, and you will find strength." in a world that constantly demands our attention and reaction, this simple truth is revolutionary. your instagram followers, others' opinions, the economy - let them go. focus on your actions, your thoughts, your responses.

2. embrace the obstacle
"the impediment to action advances action. what stands in the way becomes the way." every challenge you face is an opportunity for growth. that difficult project at work? that heartbreak? that financial setback? they're not just obstacles - they're your path to becoming stronger.

3. remember your mortality
"you could leave life right now. let that determine what you do and say and think." this isn't dark - it's clarifying. how many of our daily anxieties would evaporate if we kept this perspective? how much more boldly would we live?

but here's what marcus aurelius understood that most modern "stoic influencers" miss: these aren't just philosophical ideas - they're practical tools for living. when you're: - stuck in traffic? practice acceptance - facing criticism? focus on your response - dealing with failure? see it as a lesson

the real power of his teachings isn't in knowing them - it's in living them. in those moments when your ego is bruised, when plans fall apart, when the world seems against you - that's when these principles transform from mere words into a foundation for strength.

and perhaps most importantly, marcus teaches us that true strength isn't about dominating others or projecting an image - it's about mastering yourself. in a world obsessed with external validation, this might be his most radical message.
